Arya Jain: Shredded Letters

It is never easy to verbalize our feelings. Oftentimes, we withhold them because we're scared of being judged. Perhaps we don't want to overshare and push our loved ones away. Or, maybe, we don't want to tarnish the perfectly-curated image we've meticulously built around ourselves.

Songwriting has always been a passion of mine, but the past couple of years have accelerated that into something bigger - a floatation device when I needed it the most. It felt like the most organic way to process my emotions and express myself at a time when I needed to. Eventually it got so out of control that I wrote (and recorded) an album, where each track represents a metaphorical buried emotion, withheld feeling, or unspoken confession... a torn up letter after a spillage of ink.

Despite how intensely cathartic the record is, I had a blast bringing it to life over the past year and a half. It was less of a hobby and more of a necessity: a desire to bring closure to a convoluted chapter of my life.

And, to allow new doors to open.

Tracklist

1. Some Could Say... The opening track of the album talks about the confusion of a person's first heartbreak. For an instant, the connection felt like forever, but the narrator struggles to confront that he was blinded by hypothetical what-ifs. Distress sets in as the narrator tries to let go, realizing he will eventually be forgotten.

2. The Reality Crown After traces of denial, the narrator's emotional development allows them to analyze the relationship from an objective lens. He comes to terms with how he was trying to rewrite the script and chase an unrealistic scenario. The song details a quiet release from the relationship, understanding that it is the healthiest option.

3. i lET you HAuNt me After experiencing how hard detachment is, the narrator faces the inevitable reality of living in a world laced with corruption and evil. The song talks about losing youth and innocence when it's taken advantage of, causing a sort of paranoia.

4. The Hologram This song talks about the aftermath of recovering from someone who has caused suffering. Recovery isn't linear: the narrator starts to feel better, but one dream triggers a frenzy of insanity. The narrator tries to suppress his feelings through unhealthy coping mechanisms (taking pills). Later, a perspective shift in the bridge reveals a post-mortem to find out what caused the person to spiral.

5. Northern Lights Despite the heartbreak, life doesn't slow down for the narrator. There's this overarching pressure of performance, and how being under the lights can cause someone to bend backward. The song talks about a quiet emotional breakage while trying to people-please. Worse, the performer becomes convinced that this is his fate forever.

6. Dear Fate Track six takes the concept of performing under pressure to an extreme. Perhaps the emotional climax of the record, the narrator gives, performs, and pushes like an efficient machine - while being completely broken inside. The song reveals emotional detachment as a result of being pushed over the limit, and how breakdowns are suppressed in the presence of other people.

7. Flee The Border This song explores how past trauma can silently harm new beginnings. Although a fresh page seems promising, the narrator acknowledges that he is still influenced by his mistakes of the past. He hopes the new connection is here to stay and will help him heal, but that requires overriding his defense systems telling him to abandon it all.

8. Alicia Berenson The closing track of Shredded Letters serves as both a continuation and a culmination of the album's emotional journey. As fractures in a seemingly promising relationship are magnified by unresolved trauma, the narrator becomes consumed by the smallest details of the trainwreck - voicenotes, mixtapes, and fading promises. Comparing himself to Alicia Berenson, he ultimately vows to protect himself behind walls impenetrable by love - forever.
